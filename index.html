Yuhui Chen

Division of Psychology, Communication & Human Neuroscience
The University of Manchester

I am a fully-funded PhD researcher in Psychology at the University of Manchester, supported by the President's Doctoral Scholarship — the University's highest honour for postgraduate researchers.

My research focuses on how people regulate emotions through social interactions - a process known as extrinsic interpersonal emotion regulation - and how these processes unfold differently in individuals experiencing depression.

I am also broadly interested in human–AI interaction: how intelligent systems can be designed to support wellbeing, and what this means for real-world mental health practice and society at large.

Research Interests

I study how emotions spread and are regulated through social interaction

🧠

Interpersonal Emotion Regulation

Investigating how individuals regulate their own and others' emotions through social interactions - including various strategies and tactics - and the motivations underlying these processes.

📱

Ecological Momentary Assessment

Using intensive longitudinal methods and dynamic structural equation models (DSEM) to capture real-time affective dynamics in naturalistic settings.

💙

Depression & Emotion Regulation

Examining how interpersonal emotion regulation processes and affective dynamics differ between individuals with depression and healthy controls in daily life.

🤖

AI & Affective Science

Exploring the potential of large language models (LLMs) to understand, classify, and support interpersonal emotion regulation strategies in digital and clinical contexts.

2025
First Author Translational Issues in Psychological Science

Daily-life benefits of extrinsic interpersonal emotion regulation: An ecological momentary assessment study of depression and healthy controls

Chen, Y., Zuffianò, A., Manfredi, L., Gregori, F., Elliott, R., & López-Pérez, B. (2025)

Translational Issues in Psychological Science

Using ecological momentary assessment over 28 days, this study tracked 52 adults (26 with depression, 26 healthy controls) as they tried to comfort or uplift others in daily life. Although people with depression engaged less in this social regulation, when they did they gained greater boosts to closeness and momentary happiness than healthy controls — yet also experienced stronger emotional costs afterward, with reduced life satisfaction at later time points. The findings highlight both the promise and the complexity of social emotion regulation as a clinical target.

Co-Author Emotion

I want you to feel bad: Understanding the role of anger in extrinsic interpersonal affect worsening with ecological momentary assessment

Polias, S., Zuffianò, A., Chen, Y., Manfredi, L., Gregori, F., Bourke, L., & López-Pérez, B. (2025)

Emotion, 25(2), 320–329

When and why do people deliberately try to make others feel worse? This EMA study examined the role of anger in driving extrinsic affect worsening — intentional attempts to worsen another person's emotional state — across daily life. We found that anger predicted both the likelihood and the intensity of affect worsening attempts at the within-person level, with dispositional anger also explaining between-person differences. The findings illuminate the interpersonal dynamics of negative social emotions and the conditions under which people act on them.

Co-Author Emotion

Exploring the Potential of Large Language Models to Understand Interpersonal Emotion Regulation Strategies From Narratives

López-Pérez, B., Chen, Y., Li, X., Cheng, S., & Razavi, P. (2025)

Emotion (Washington, D.C.), 25(7), 1653–1667

Can large language models replace human coders in identifying emotion regulation strategies from narratives? Across two studies (N = 2,824 and N = 2,090 responses), this paper evaluated ChatGPT and Claude against human coding of interpersonal emotion regulation strategies. After prompt refinement, both LLMs achieved substantial agreement with human coders (κ > .76), though accuracy varied notably by emotion and strategy type — pointing to both the promise of LLM-assisted coding and the critical importance of prompt engineering and validation.

Co-Author Cognition & Emotion

Distraction over reappraisal strategies in interpersonal emotion regulation: associations with emotional difficulties

López-Pérez, B., Kwon, K., & Chen, Y. (2025)

Cognition and Emotion, 40(2), 345–359

Is it better to distract someone from their feelings or help them reframe the situation? This paper examines whether a dominant tendency to use distraction — rather than reappraisal — when regulating others' emotions is linked to emotional difficulties. In Study 1 (N = 274), greater reliance on distraction over reappraisal predicted more difficulties in identifying and describing feelings and higher personal distress. Study 2 (N = 121) used latent class analysis to identify three regulator profiles: consistent reappraisal users (23%), affective engagement users (48%), and distraction users (19%) — with the distraction class scoring higher on emotion identification difficulties and experiential avoidance. The findings suggest that habitual use of diversion strategies to regulate others' emotions may reflect broader emotional difficulties in the regulator themselves.

Manuscripts Under Review

  • Chen, Y., López-Pérez, B., & Walker, S. A. (2025). A Digital Shoulder to Cry On: Understanding Why Large Language Models Can Be Effective in Extrinsic Interpersonal Emotion Regulation. Under Review.

    Across five studies, this research compared LLM- versus human-generated comforting messages and found that LLMs can replicate — and in some contexts surpass — human responses in regulating others' emotions. Crucially, actionable support (specific, implementable advice) emerged as the key ingredient for emotional improvement, regardless of whether the message came from an AI or a person. The findings have implications for AI-assisted emotional support and theories of interpersonal emotion regulation.
